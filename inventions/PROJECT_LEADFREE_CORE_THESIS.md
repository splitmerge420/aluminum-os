## The Problem

Iron deficiency affects approximately 2 billion people globally. The existing blood product industry — collection, screening, fractionation, cold chain storage, distribution — is one of the most expensive and infrastructure-dependent supply chains in medicine. Access is gatekept by regulatory frameworks, hospital systems, and a plasma fractionation industry dominated by a handful of corporations (CSL Behring, Grifols, Takeda).

For the Global South, this infrastructure largely doesn't exist. People die from treatable iron deficiency because the delivery mechanism — medical-grade blood products — requires an entire healthcare system to function.

---

## The Insight

Human hemoglobin is a protein. Proteins can be produced by engineered yeast via fermentation. The gene sequence for human hemoglobin is publicly available.

The molecule produced by yeast fermentation is molecularly identical to the hemoglobin in human blood. It was never inside a human body. It is a fermentation product — the same category as beer, bread, or soy sauce.

---

## The Regulatory Arbitrage

The regulatory framework distinguishes between:

1. **Human-derived biological products** (blood, plasma, tissue) — regulated by FDA CBER, requires blood bank licensing, donor screening, cold chain, expiration dating, medical oversight.

2. **Fermentation-derived proteins sold as food ingredients** (flavoring, supplements) — regulated by FDA CFSAN under GRAS or food additive frameworks.

The same molecule falls into completely different regulatory categories depending on whether it came from a human vein or a yeast tank.

Synthetic human hemoglobin produced by fermentation has never been inside a human body. It is not a blood product. It is a protein ingredient produced by microorganisms, identical in regulatory category to the soy leghemoglobin already approved as GRAS for use in Impossible Foods products.

## The Disruption

The entire blood product supply chain exists because the molecule cannot be manufactured outside the human body. Synthetic biology removes that constraint.

At commodity fermentation pricing, the cost of producing human-identical hemoglobin drops from the medical blood product price point (hundreds to thousands of dollars) to food ingredient pricing (single digits).

**Iron deficiency for 2 billion people becomes a food problem, not a medical problem.** Food problems are solvable with agriculture-scale infrastructure. Medical problems require hospital-scale infrastructure. The Global South has farms. It does not have blood banks.

## The Regulatory Question (Unresolved — Critical Path)

Key questions for regulatory counsel:
1. Does FDA distinguish between non-human proteins (soy leghemoglobin) and human-identical proteins (synthetic human hemoglobin) produced by the same fermentation process?
2. Does GRAS self-determination apply, or is a food additive petition required for a human-identical protein?
3. Are there biosafety or dual-use concerns with manufacturing human proteins at industrial scale outside medical oversight?
4. How do international regulatory frameworks (EU Novel Foods, Codex Alimentarius) treat this category?
